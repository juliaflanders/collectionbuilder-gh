## Tesselation

The word "tesselation" literally refers to tiles: flat shapes that divide a plane. Mathematical ideas about tesselation emphasize the regularity of tiles, their geometrical precision and repetition, and the distinctive types of patterns that are possible with a specific repertoire of shapes and colors. Natural forms also tesselate, or seem to do so when viewed from a particular angle. The "plane" that is tesselated is often arbitrary, an artifact of the viewing angle, a whim or design of the photographer. I use the term loosely, to capture the visual effect of segmentation or repeated shapes, even if they don't strictly tesselate in a geometrical sense. 

Tesselation appeals to me because of its visual rhythm and the dynamic it sets up between the individual tiny shapes and the larger pattern they create. In some cases there's an effect almost of a fractal, with tesselation and repetition taking place at different levels of scale.

## Examples

Many living forms have a natural inclination to tesselate, because the shape they take is guided by their search for things like light or nutrients, or by the need to protect the organism. Leaves grow into spaces to capture sunlight; bark forms tiles by cracking while still covering the tree's surface. 

{% include feature/image.html objectid="coll016;coll012;coll037" %}

Inorganic forms tesselate when a surface is transected by crystal structures, light rays, or disturbed by rhythmic motion (such as a wave form). Water crystals are particularly generative: they form on bodies of water large and small, but also in mud and sand slurries where they create sculptural effects. On surfaces like wood, glass, and metal, with a very slow freezing process, they sometimes form elaborate flowers and delicate traceries.
